## Overall Findings

**Finding F-A (scene sacrifice)**: H1 PASS. Crop-only references reliably sacrifice scene context when the hero shot extends beyond the crop bounds. The small crop lost all three scene elements (0/3). The large crop recovered the barometer via overlap of the shelving area but still missed sconces and only partially caught windows. This effect is not prompt-dependent — the sparse text is identical across all variants.

**Finding F-B (dual-ref advantage)**: H2 PASS. Dual-ref with small crop (3D-small) is the top-performing variant overall, achieving the only perfect composite score (2.000). The full-scene ref provides the scene context the crop lacks, while the crop provides orrery asymmetry anchoring. Dual-ref resolves the scene-sacrifice of crop-only without sacrificing target fidelity.

**Finding F-C (crop resolution)**: H3 PASS. Crop resolution is a material variable for target element fidelity in single-ref context. A 353×512px crop dropped D1 from 4.0 to 1.0. The small crop was insufficient to convey the Saturn arm's position and direction, and failed to anchor the sun's off-center placement. Minimum ~2048px appears strongly warranted for complex multi-arm instruments.

**Finding F-D (dual-ref robustness)**: H4 PARTIAL. Dual-ref is fully robust to crop size on D1 (target element fidelity) — both 3D variants achieved 4.0. However, dual-ref is NOT fully robust on D2 (scene context): 3D-large missed SE2 (amber sconce) while 3D-small captured it. This is a puzzling result — 3D-large should have equal or better scene access since the full-scene ref is identical for both. The sconce omission in 3D-large may reflect stochastic variation or compositional competition from the larger crop dominating the model's scene construction. This finding warrants a follow-up trial to determine whether this gap is reproducible or noise.

## Judge Notes

### Observations on D1

The most striking D1 finding is the near-total failure of 3A-small. At 353×512px, the small crop successfully conveyed the orrery's existence and general form but could not reliably encode the Saturn arm (F2) or the sun's off-center position (F4). These are radially-positioned features that may require more frame context to unambiguously represent directionality. Conversely, 3B (full-scene ref only) achieved 3.5/4, losing only partial credit on F4. The full scene's lower orrery resolution did not prevent the model from reproducing the asymmetric arm configuration — suggesting the directional asymmetry can be encoded even at reduced apparent size within a wider scene.

### Observations on D2

3D-small's perfect D2 (3.0) and 3D-large's D2 of 2.0 is the trial's most unexpected result. The full-scene ref image (shot1-full.jpeg) is identical for both — so the sconce dropout in 3D-large cannot be attributed to the scene ref. The only variable is the crop. The large crop (2048×1834px) shows the orrery + hands + table in high detail; it is possible the model allocated greater "attention weight" to reconstructing the foreground from the large crop, leaving less capacity to pull scene elements from the full-scene ref. The small crop, being compact and quickly processed, may leave more capacity for the full-scene ref to populate the background. This is a testable hypothesis and would justify a targeted S04b follow-up.

### Observations on D3

The D3 ranking reveals a non-obvious result: 3D-small (small crop + full-scene) outperforms 3D-large (large crop + full-scene). In single-ref context, the larger crop was dramatically better (3A-large D3=1.500 vs 3A-small D3=0.250). In dual-ref context, the advantage reverses slightly. This interaction effect — crop size × ref strategy — is the most pedagogically interesting finding of the trial.

### Edge cases or ambiguities encountered

1. **F4 (sun off-center) in 3B**: The sun sphere's base platform position is hard to evaluate in a medium close-up hero shot where the base's full diameter is not always visible. 3B's partial score reflects that the sun is not clearly right-of-center; it could be central. This is an inherent limitation of medium close-up framing for this sub-element.

2. **SE3 in 3A-large**: The upper-left corner shows an arch form, but it is extremely partial — only the right leg of one arch is visible. Scored partial per criteria ("window or light source visible but arch form unclear"). If scored no, 3A-large D2 would drop to 1.0.

3. **3D-large SE2**: Right wall visible, no sconce. This was unexpected given 3D-small had a clear sconce. Scored no. If this is stochastic noise, the H4 result could shift to pass in a repeat trial.

### Skeptic adversarial notes

- **3A-small F1 and F3 scored partial rather than no**: The tilt direction and arm curve direction are genuinely ambiguous at the crop's viewing angle and resolution. A skeptic could argue these should be scored no, which would push 3A-small D1 to 0.0 and make H3 even more decisive (4.0 vs 0.0). The partial scores are conservative/generous to 3A-small.
- **3B F4 scored partial**: A skeptic could argue the sun is clearly positioned sufficiently right-of-center for a yes. If scored yes, 3B D1 = 4.0 and D3 = 1.875 → 2.000, tying 3D-small at the top. However, on careful examination the off-center positioning is notably less clear than in 3A-large or the 3D variants, so partial is defensible.
- **H4 partial vs fail**: Skeptic may note the D2 gap between 3D variants (1.0 point) is just at the threshold boundary (threshold is 0.5, gap is 1.0). This is unambiguously above threshold, so partial is the correct verdict.
- **The 3D-large SE2 dropout deserves scrutiny**: A single missing sconce is the difference between H4 partial and H4 pass. This finding is suggestive but not conclusive — a single-generation trial cannot distinguish systematic effect from stochastic noise.
